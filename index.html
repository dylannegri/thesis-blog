Dylan Negri is currently a candidate for a degree from the MFA Design and Technology program at Parsons. He has a background in Sculpture and now has an invested interest in product design, interactive design, games, installation and understanding how interfaces affect us in our daily lives.

Potential Area of Research:

OBSOLESCENCE

  1. An object that is informed by this research to represent a ‘timeless’ design.

  2. A game that teaches us about the usefulness, or lack thereof, of any and/or all things.

  3. An interactive space that gives an experience of how time passes by.

TRADITIONAL CRAFT

  1. Create a series of hand crafted (high end) products that modernize traditional craft with technology.

  2. Create a VR experience for prototyping these products to give the viewer an idea of how it would feel to live with these kinds of objects.

  3. Use VR or AR to educate individuals on the benefits of gaining craft skills.

UBIQUITOUS OBJECTS

  1. Craft a series of products that appear like one ubiquitous object but functions like another; creating a fantastical experience.

  2. Create a virtual space that gamifies these objects giving them an additional function beyond their traditional one.

  3. Making a game that askes the player to figure out what the function of a particular object is.

Abstract Statement:

Technology is always changing. While some things have been more resilient to time than others, such as the QWERTY keyboard, with modern technology we have moved away from button interfaces toward screen based capacitive touch. For my thesis, I want to observe the practicalities of physical interfaces and consider what the future may have in store for them. My intention is to invoke a conversation between physical and digital interactions: so that we might establish an argument for why we need to maintain tangible things. Otherwise, we may end up changing fundamental ways in which we perceive and interact with the world. As physical beings, we enjoy interacting with tangible things that give us physical feedback; which in many cases, is good for our psychological well-being because of the joy that can come from a good button press. We risk unlearning how to utilize these types of interfaces entirely, causing use to be even less familiar with how and why the world functions as it does.
